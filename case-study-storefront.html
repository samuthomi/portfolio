Seller Storefront

Designing a self-checkout storefront sellers would trust and actually use

Role: Research, UX Design, UI Design and UX Writing Tools: Figma, Miro Team: 1 UX designer, 6 developers Timeline: Sep – Dec 2023 (16 weeks)

Context and stakes

A year after launching Sendy Fulfillment, sellers loved storing stock at fulfillment centers because it reduced delivery lead times and simplified operations. That success exposed the next opportunity: help sellers move from "stored inventory" to completed sales, without extra manual work.

The idea was a self-checkout storefront: sellers publish a simple web page showing items available in the warehouse, customers purchase directly, and Sendy handles delivery—automating the sales loop end-to-end.

What was actually blocking progress

This wasn't a "design a storefront page" problem. The risk was shipping a storefront that didn't match how sellers build trust, price products, and close payments in the real world.

We needed to validate:

  • Would sellers trust Sendy to collect payments?
  • What payment options were non-negotiable?
  • What must be configurable for sellers to run a real business?
  • What is the smallest product that still works in-market?

Approach: validate direction before we build

Because this was a new product concept, we started with a demo-first approach—prototype quickly, test with existing sellers, then let that feedback define what mattered for a first release.

Demo 1 — Early signal, fast learning

I built a rough mobile concept and turned it into an interactive prototype to test with five sellers.

What we learned:

  • Setup + checkout felt intuitive, but sellers wanted fewer steps.
  • Sellers were excited about Sendy collecting payments through the storefront.
  • Some wanted deeper branding/customization (background images, custom colors).

What changed after Demo 1:

  • We tightened the flow around momentum: publish/share sooner, configure later.
  • We treated "payment collection" as a value anchor worth designing around.

Demo 2 — Higher fidelity, real constraints

With more confidence in the direction, we tested a higher-fidelity prototype with 20 participants to shape the backlog for a first release.

Key insights that reshaped the product:

  • Payment on Delivery (cash or mobile money) was crucial for customer trust.
  • Sellers wanted flexible price configuration to pass fulfilment/delivery fees to customers (but it would require more dev time).
  • Promotions mattered: discounts and bundling to drive sales.
  • Sellers needed low stock notifications to manage demand and avoid failures.

Key decisions

This project became a sequence of product decisions, not a UI exercise:

Optimize for seller momentum, not completeness — Reduce steps and get sellers to a shareable storefront quickly.

Treat trust mechanisms as core — Payment on Delivery wasn't a "phase two" feature—it was foundational to adoption.

Separate "must-have" from "expensive-to-build" — Pricing configuration was important, but we acknowledged the dev cost and planned it intentionally rather than sneaking it into v1.

Bake growth into the product — Discounts and bundling weren't extras—they were how sellers sell.

Final design solution

We finalized the design and handed off for development. The solution included:

  • A desktop + mobile storefront where customers can browse and place orders.
  • A new Storefront tab inside the Sendy Fulfillment app to manage catalog, customize the page, and share product/store links.

Outcome

The work was handed off, but it was never released due to layoffs and shifting business priorities.

Even without launch, we validated what would make a storefront viable in-market—trust, payment expectations, seller pricing needs, and lightweight setup—before investing heavily in build.

Reflections

Involving customers early and often helped us focus on problems that were meaningful to sellers—and avoid building something that looked good but wouldn't be adopted.
